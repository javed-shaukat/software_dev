About Geography of Pakistan

Pakistan is strategically located in South Asia, bordered by:

  • India to the east.
  • Afghanistan and Iran to the west.
  • China to the north.
  • The Arabian Sea to the south.

The country features one of the world's most diverse landscapes, ranging from the soaring peaks of the Karakoram to the golden beaches along the Arabian Sea.

Cultural Significance

Pakistan's culture is a rich tapestry woven from:

  1. Ancient Indus Valley Civilization (3300-1300 BCE)
  2. Gandhara Buddhist heritage
  3. Islamic influences dating back to the 8th century
  4. Mughal Empire's architectural and artistic legacy
  5. British colonial period influences

This unique blend creates a cultural experience unlike any other in the world.

What Pakistan is Famous For

  • Five of the world's 14 eight-thousanders.
  • The world's highest concentration of high mountains.

Historical Heritage

UNESCO World Heritage Sites:

  • Mohenjo-Daro

    • Mohenjo-Daro is an archaeological site in Sindh, Pakistan, built in 2500 BCE. Mohenjo-Daro is primarily known for being one of the largest and best-preserved cities of the Indus Valley Civilization, and for its advanced urban planning and engineering. The site's sophisticated infrastructure, including organized drainage systems, sewage systems, and public baths, are particularly notable.

  • Taxila

    • Taxila, located in the Rawalpindi district of Pakistan's Punjab province, is a vast serial site that includes a Mesolithic cave and the archaeological remains of four early settlement sites, Buddhist monasteries, and a Muslim mosque and madrassa.

  • Shalimar Gardens

    • The Shalimar Gardens is well known for chini khanas, or arched niches, behind garden waterfalls. They are a unique feature in the Bagh. These niches were lighted at night with oil lamps, which gave a fairy tale appearance to the water falls.

  • Makli Necropolis

    • Makli Necropolis is located in the town of Makli, which is located on a plateau approximately 6 kilometres from the city of Thatta, the capital of lower Sindh until the 17th century. The vast necropolis of Makli is among the largest in the world. Kings, queens, governors, saints, scholars, and philosophers are buried here in brick or stone monuments, some of which are lavishly decorated with glazed tiles.
